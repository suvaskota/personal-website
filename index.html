Power in Being Concise

The way our education system is currently structured, we have been groomed to beat around the bush and fluff in our writing. Some examples including reaching a word or page count for a paper. Now I agree that in the beginning, including these benchmarks is necessary to develop depth in students' writing. However, sometimes in higher levels of academia, even if the quality of a paper is really good, failing to meet the page limit can effect your grade. As a result, students are motivated to include lots of fluff in their writing, even when there is no need to, in order to meet these requirements. While adding fluff may work for grades etc, this habit can be hindering and inefficient in the real world. I personally realized this when I was writing essays for college.

My original drafts of college essays would always be well over the word count, but interestingly, I could slash up to 200 words and still retain all the points I wanted to get across with the intended effect and power. There is power in being concise. The readers or audience will not get bored, we save time for all parties involved, and our intended points will always be more powerful and direct when there is less fluff surrounding them. The same can be applied to meetings as well. Especially in our increasingly busy lives, people would really appreciate meetings that are short, straight to the point and skip all the formalities. The average attention span of a person in a meeting is 20 minutes so if you are unnecessarily stretching a meeting longer than it has to be, then you are trading off productivity and time that could have been used for something more beneficial. Congress is a perfect example of how people use fluff to prevent things from being done. So bottom line, if you want to get stuff done and have a powerful effect, be concise and to the point!
